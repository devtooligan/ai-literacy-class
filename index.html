ü§ñ So‚Ä¶ What Is an LLM?

Before we talk about how to write great prompts, let's zoom out a bit and make sure we understand what we're actually working with.

LLM stands for Large Language Model.
These models ‚Äî like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini ‚Äî are trained on a massive amount of text to do one simple thing: ‚úÖ Predict the next token.

üß± What's a Token?

LLMs don't see full words. They break everything into smaller pieces called tokens ‚Äî which might be whole words, syllables, or even fragments of words. One way to think of it is it's usually around 4 characters.

üß† What Does It Mean to Predict the Next Token?

LLMs work by guessing what comes next ‚Äî one token at a time. It's like autocomplete on steroids.

For example: If I say "The capital of France is ___" ‚Äî what do you think the next word should be?

Paris? Correct! You just predicted the next token.

This is exactly what language models are trained to do ‚Äî predict the next token based on everything that came before it.

üß† Do Chatbots Have Memory?

When ChatGPT first launched, it had no memory at all. Every time you typed something, it completely forgot what you'd said before.

Today, tools like ChatGPT can simulate memory ‚Äî but it's not the kind humans have. Instead, they "remember" by looking at previous messages in the current conversation.

üëâ DO THIS NOW: The Memory Experiment

  1. Go to ChatGPT and start a fresh new chat session, ask: "What's my favorite food?"
  2. Now tell it: "My favorite food is pizza."
  3. Then ask again: "What's my favorite food?"
  4. Finally, ask: "How do you remember things I told you earlier?"

What's really happening here? When you ask "What's my favorite food?" the second time, ChatGPT isn't pulling from long-term memory ‚Äî it's just re-reading the conversation.

User: What's my favorite food?
ChatGPT: I'm not sure ‚Äî you haven't told me yet!
User: My favorite food is pizza.
User: What's my favorite food?

You can see how this works ‚Äî just like a person reading a conversation transcript, the AI answers based on what's written above.

But there's a limit to how much the AI can "see" at once. That's where the concept of a context window comes in.

üß† What's a Context Window?

The context window is like the AI's short-term memory. It determines how many tokens (bits of text) the model can consider at once. When a conversation gets too long, older messages start to drop off the back.

üß© Before You Start Researching: The 3 Keys to a Great Prompt

Before you dive into research with ChatGPT, here's one of the most powerful ideas in this course: Every great prompt has three parts: Role, Task, and Output.

This structure helps the AI respond clearly, accurately, and in the tone you want.

Let's break it down ‚Äî and try a quick experiment along the way.

1. üßë‚Äçüè´ Role ‚Äì Who is the AI pretending to be?

LLMs mirror the tone and style you give them. If you say: "You're a terrible scientist," it will try to sound like one!

üëâ DO THIS NOW:

Open a fresh ChatGPT window and run these 3 prompts (start a new chat for each one):

  • üü° Prompt 1: "Tell me about cats."
  • üî¥ Prompt 2: "You don't know much about animals and you're a poor communicator. Tell me about cats."
  • üü¢ Prompt 3: "You are the world's leading expert on cats and an excellent communicator. Tell me about cats."

Same question ‚Äî wildly different results. Like we talked about above, LLMs predict the next token. So if you tell it that it's a poor communicator, it will predict what a poor communicator would say. If you tell it that it's an expert, it will predict what an expert will say.

2. üéØ Task ‚Äì What exactly do you want it to do?

Be specific. "Tell me about finance" is too vague. Try something like:
"Explain the basics of stock trading to someone with no background in finance, using simple examples." The more information you give it about your expectations and what you want and what exactly you want it to do, the better the outcome will be.

3. üìù Output ‚Äì What format should the answer take?

If you don't give the model structure, it will guess ‚Äî and that doesn't always work. Try prompts like:

  • "Give me a 1-paragraph summary."
  • "Explain it like I'm 5 (ELI5)."
  • "List the key points as bullet points."
  • "Give me a 1-sentence summary, then a paragraph, then a detailed outline."

Personally, I love the "zoom in" approach ‚Äî asking for a summary, then expanding step by step.

  • Size in the frame ‚Äî make your subject big and bold, or small within the scene

2Ô∏è‚É£ The Director's Toolkit

Think Like a Film Director

Video builds on everything you learned about photography in Week 2, but now we add the magic of movement. Let's break down the key elements you control:

üéØ SUBJECT - Who or What is Your Focus

This is still the star of your scene, just like in photography. But now you can think about:

  • Expression and emotion: happy, serious, surprised, confused, determined
  • What they're doing: walking, running, sitting, working, playing
  • How they move: slowly, quickly, gracefully, clumsily
  • Where they look: at the camera, off in the distance, at something specific

üí° LIGHT - Setting the Mood

Lighting completely changes how your video feels. You can specify:

  • Time of day: "golden hour sunset," "harsh midday sun," "soft morning light"
  • Weather: "overcast and moody," "bright and sunny," "dramatic storm clouds"
  • Artificial lighting: "neon city lights," "warm fireplace glow," "stark fluorescent lighting"
  • Direction: "backlit," "side lighting," "spotlit from above"

Think about how each makes you feel: warm sunset light feels cozy, while harsh fluorescent feels cold or clinical.

üî≠ DISTANCE - How Close Are We?

This controls the emotional connection and what information we see:

  • Wide shot: Shows the whole scene, makes the person feel small or part of a bigger world
  • Medium shot: Shows the person from waist up, good for conversations or activities
  • Close-up: Shows just the face or hands, creates intimacy and emotion
  • Extreme close-up: Shows tiny details like eyes or textures, very dramatic

üé¨ MOVEMENT - The New Dimension!

This is what makes video special. You control both camera movement and subject movement:

Camera movements:

  • Dolly in: Camera moves closer (builds tension, draws focus)
  • Dolly out: Camera moves away (reveals context, creates distance)
  • Pan left/right: Camera swivels horizontally (follows action, reveals new info)
  • Orbit: Camera circles around the subject (dynamic, shows all angles)
  • Track alongside: Camera moves parallel with a moving subject

Subject movements:

  • Walking, running, dancing, working
  • Turning their head, reaching for something
  • Sitting down, standing up, leaning forward

üëâ DO THIS NOW - The Director's Experiment:

  1. Change the lighting: Add "golden hour lighting" or "dramatic shadows" to your prompt
  2. Change the distance: Try "close-up" or "wide shot"
  3. Change the movement: Add "dolly in slowly" or "camera pans right"

Questions to consider:

  • How does each change affect the feeling?
  • Which version tells the story better?
  • What surprises you about the AI's interpretation?

üèÜ Grandmaster Assignment

The Kuleshov Effect shows that the shots before and after a neutral image change how we perceive emotion in that image.

üëâ DO THIS NOW:

    • A mysterious/suspenseful scene (dark hallway, locked door, shadows)
